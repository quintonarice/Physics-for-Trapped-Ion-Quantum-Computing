Chapter 1: Postulates of Quantum Mechanics

1. Motivation: Why Quantum?

You may recall images of the earliest classical computers—refrigerator-sized machines built from thousands of vacuum tubes. Each vacuum tube functioned as a simple on/off switch: electrons flowed to the anode (“on”) or were blocked (“off”). Later, transistors replaced vacuum tubes, shrinking switching elements from inches, to millimeters, to nanometers.

As transistor dimensions approached the scale of only a few dozen atoms, unavoidable quantum effects—tunneling, interference, and discrete energy levels—began to dominate. At that point, it became clear that classical on/off logic would no longer scale cleanly. Instead of fighting quantum mechanics, physicists embraced it as a resource. Thus began the modern pursuit of quantum computation.

Classical bits take definite values 0 or 1. Quantum bits (qubits) may exist in superpositions of 0 and 1 prior to measurement. Understanding what “state,” “superposition,” and “measurement” mean requires the formal structure of quantum mechanics. We begin with the Postulates.

2. Postulate 1 — Quantum States

Postulate 1 states:

An isolated physical system is associated with a complex vector space with an inner product (the Hilbert space). The state of the system is represented by a unit vector in this space.

2.1 Vectors, inner products, and quantum states

In ordinary 2D real space, a vector is written using components

$$ \vec{v} = v_x \hat{x} + v_y \hat{y}. $$

As a column matrix:

$$ \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}. $$

Quantum states use the same idea, but now the components may be complex numbers. The state vector is written using Dirac notation:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}, $$ with \(a, b \in \mathbb{C}\).

2.2 The inner product (bra–ket)

The quantum inner product is the generalization of the dot product. For two states \( |\phi\rangle \) and \( |\psi\rangle \),

$$ \langle \phi | \psi \rangle = \phi_1^* \psi_1 + \phi_2^* \psi_2. $$

Here, the “bra” \( \langle \phi | \) is the conjugate-transpose of the ket \( |\phi\rangle \):

$$ \langle \phi | = (|\phi\rangle)^\dagger. $$

The normalization condition from Postulate 1 is:

$$ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1. $$

This guarantees that quantum states are unit vectors, preserving probabilistic interpretation.

Compute \( |\psi\rangle = (1+i,\, 2)^T \) and \( |\phi\rangle = (2,\, -i)^T \). Find \( \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \).

3. Example: The Qubit

A qubit is a two-dimensional quantum system with orthonormal basis states:

$$ |0\rangle = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}, \qquad |1\rangle = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}. $$

A general qubit is written as:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle, $$ with normalization: $$ |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1. $$

Because \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) may be complex, the qubit has richer structure than a classical bit.

Determine whether \( |\psi\rangle = 3|0\rangle + 4|1\rangle \) is a valid quantum state.

4. Complex Amplitudes and the Bloch Parameterization

Write the amplitudes in polar form:

$$ \alpha = r_0 e^{i\phi_0}, \qquad \beta = r_1 e^{i\phi_1}. $$

After factoring out a global phase (physically irrelevant), the qubit becomes:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|0\rangle + e^{i\phi} \sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|1\rangle, $$ where $$ \phi = \phi_1 - \phi_0. $$

This form corresponds to spherical coordinates on the Bloch sphere. The angles have the conventional meaning:

  • \(\theta = 0\) → north pole → \( |0\rangle \)
  • \(\theta = \pi\) → south pole → \( |1\rangle \)
  • \(\phi\) → rotation around the z-axis

Although \(\phi\) originates from a complex phase difference, it corresponds physically to a rotation about the z-axis. This is what makes the Bloch sphere a powerful visualization tool.

Verify that \( |\psi\rangle = \cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle \) is automatically normalized.

5. Postulate 2 — Unitary Time Evolution

Postulate 2 states:

The evolution of a closed quantum system is described by a unitary operator.

If the system starts in \( |\psi(0)\rangle \), then after time \(t\):

$$ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t)\,|\psi(0)\rangle. $$

Unitarity preserves the inner product:

$$ U^\dagger U = I. $$

5.1 Pauli matrices as basic unitary operations

Three fundamental unitaries are the Pauli operators:

$$ X = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{bmatrix}0 & -i \\ i & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1\end{bmatrix}. $$

Their actions on a general qubit \( |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \) are:

$$ X|\psi\rangle = \alpha|1\rangle + \beta|0\rangle, $$ $$ Y|\psi\rangle = i\alpha|1\rangle - i\beta|0\rangle, $$ $$ Z|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle - \beta|1\rangle. $$
  • X performs a bit flip.
  • Y performs a bit + phase flip.
  • Z performs a phase flip.
Verify that \(X^\dagger X = Y^\dagger Y = Z^\dagger Z = I\).

6. Summary

In this chapter, we introduced:

  • The meaning of a quantum state and the inner-product structure behind it.
  • The qubit as the simplest quantum system.
  • The Bloch-sphere parameterization and its geometric interpretation.
  • Unitary evolution and the fundamental role of operators such as the Pauli matrices.

These ingredients are essential for understanding how trapped-ion qubits behave and how they are manipulated in real quantum computers. In the next chapters, we will build step-by-step toward full single- and two-qubit control.
