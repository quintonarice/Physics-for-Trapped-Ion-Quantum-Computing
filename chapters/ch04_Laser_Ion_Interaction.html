Laser-Ion Interaction: Coupling Internal States to Motion

The Lamb-Dicke Parameter

Consider the general form of the propagating electric field of a laser:

\[ \ \mathbf{E} (\mathbf{r},t) \propto \cos (\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t) \tag{4.1} \]

where $\mathbf{k}$ is the wave vector, $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector, and $\omega$ is the laser frequency. We start by considering axial coupling only which means that $\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} = kz$. And since we know that in quantum mechanics the position is an operator, $z \rightarrow \hat Z$, then:

\[ \ \mathbf{E} (\hat Z,t) \propto \cos (k_z \hat Z - \omega t) \tag{4.2} \]

Now, for a charge distribution in an external electric field, the interaction energy is given from classical electromagnetism:

\[ \ U = - \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{E} \tag{4.3} \]

where $\mathbf{p}$ is the electric dipole moment. Since the electron cloud surrounding the atom moves in response to the external electric field we express the dipole moment as $\mathbf{p} = -e \mathbf{r}_e$ where $e$ is the charge and $\mathbf{r}_e$ is the electrons position relative to the nucleus. In quantum mechanics, the dipole moment becomes an operator acting on only the internal electronic states (for formal proof see Appendix), while the electric field must be evaluated at the ion’s position operator. The resulting time-dependent interaction Hamiltonian therefore couples the ion’s internal states to its quantized motional states.

\[ \ \hat H_{int} (t) = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(k_z \hat Z - \omega t) \tag{4.4} \]

where $\mathbf{E}_0$ is the electric field amplitude. Now recall the expression for the position operator $\hat Z$ from the last section.

\[ \ \hat Z = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger \big) \tag{4.5} \]

So that:

\[ \ \hat H_{int} (t) = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos \big (k_z \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger \big ) - \omega t \big ) \tag{4.6} \]

We now define the Lamb-Dicke parameter as:

\[ \ \eta_z \equiv k_z \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \tag{4.7} \]

In what's known as the Lamb-Dicke regime the ion's spatial extent is much smaller than the laser wavelength $\lambda$ and since $k_z \sim \frac{1}{\lambda}$, it follows that in the Lamb-Dicke regime:

\[ \ \eta_z \ll 1 \tag{4.8} \]

Under this approximation, we may expand the cosine term of the interaction Hamiltonian as:

\[ \ \cos \big( \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) - \omega t \big) \approx \cos(\omega t) - \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t) + \mathcal O (\eta_z^2) \tag{4.9} \]

where $\mathcal O(\eta_z^2)$ represents higher order terms in the expansion. The zeroth-order term, $\cos(\omega t)$, corresponds to laser-driven internal transitions that do not affect the ion’s motional state. Note that motion is invisible to the laser, the ion behaves point-like, and the motional quantum number $n$ does not change. This is known as the carrier transition. The first-order term, $ \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t)$ accounts for the finite spatial extent of the ion’s motion and allows the laser to exchange energy with the motional degree of freedom. We already know that $\hat a^\dagger$ adds one phonon and $\hat a$ removes one phonon. This terms also informs us that the internal and motional energies are coupled. Additionally, the creation and annihilation of phonons correspond to what are known as blue and red sidebands, respectively. We will explore these in detail next. Lastly, the higher-order terms, $\mathcal O (\eta_z^2)$ correspond to multi-phonon processes and are suppressed by higher powers of $\eta_z$ in the Lamb–Dicke regime.

At this stage, the physical origin of the carrier and sideband transitions can be understood directly from the expanded interaction Hamiltonian. However, to fully justify the resonance conditions and the emergence of the effective Hamiltonians used throughout trapped-ion physics, one may solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation explicitly.

The detailed perturbative Schrodinger picutre derivation is included at the end of this section for completeness. The next section discusses Two-Level Systems in the interaction picture which extends nicely to multi-ion systems.

Two-Level Systems

In this section, we go into a detailed derviation of the carrier transition and the red/blue sideband transitions to fully understand their origin (which technically is just the interaction Hamiltonian). It is highly encouraged to the reader to slow down and perform all necessary steps to reach the final result. We begin by considering the Schrodinger Equation of Postulate 2:

\[ \ i \hbar \frac{d}{dt} | \psi(t) \rangle = \hat H (t) |\psi (t) \rangle \tag{4.10} \]

We know invoke Time-dependent Perturbation Theory where we split the Hamiltonian into a time-independent part, $\hat H_0$, and a time-dependent part, $\hat H_{int}(t)$, so that:

\[ \ \hat H(t) = \hat H_0 + \hat H_{int} (t) \tag{4.11} \]

We see that only the interaction term can change both internal or motional quantum states. Now, by plugging equation 4.11 into equation 4.10:

\[ \begin{align} \ i \hbar \frac{d}{dt}| \psi(t) \rangle & = (\hat H_0 + \hat H_{int} (t)) |\psi(t)\rangle \\ & = \hat H_0 |\psi(t)\rangle + \hat H_{int}(t) |\psi(t)\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.12} \]

Integrating both sides from $0$ to $t$ gives:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.13} \]

Focusing on just the first integral, equations 1.60 and 1.62 allows us to write the solution as:

\[ \ |\psi(t')\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle + \mathcal O (\hat H_{int}) \tag{4.14} \]

Therefore,

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle dt' \tag{4.15} \]

From the Schrodinger equation, $\hat H_0 e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} = i \hbar \frac{d}{dt'} |\psi(t')\rangle$, so:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t i \hbar \frac{d}{dt'} |\psi(t')\rangle |\psi(0)\rangle dt' \tag{4.16} \]

Which upon integrating gives:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{4.17} \]

So, from equation 4.13, we have:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.18} \]

Leading to:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.19} \]

Now we can tackle the second integral which is much more formidable.

The interaction Hamiltonian under our approximation in equation 4.9 to first order becomes:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_{int} (t) & = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 [\cos(\omega t) - \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t)] \\ & = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t) + \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t) + \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.20} \]

We now have 3 separate integrals to evaluate:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')|\psi(t')\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')|\psi(t')\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.21} \]

Now since $|\psi(t') = e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle$, the integral is written:

\[ \ = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.22} \]

We now take the intial state $|\psi(0)\rangle$ to be composite of electronic ground and motional states, $|g\rangle$ and $|n\rangle$, so that $|\psi(0)\rangle = |g,n\rangle$ where $|g,n \rangle$ is the tensor product from Posutlate 4: $|g,n\rangle = |g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle$. Therefore,

\[ \ = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.23} \]

It is now important to recognize the action of $e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle$ because it appears in each term. We first write the unperturbed Hamiltonian, $\hat H_0$ as the sum of an electronic Hamiltonian, $\hat H_{el}$, and a motional Hamiltonian, $\hat H_{mot}$, so that:

\[ \ \hat H_0 = \hat H_{el} + \hat H_{mot} \tag{4.24} \]

We already know that:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_{el} |g\rangle & = E_g |g\rangle \\ \hat H_{mot} |n\rangle & = \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})|n\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.25} \]

So, explicitly,

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_0 |g,n\rangle & = (\hat H_{el} + \hat H_{mot})(|g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle)\\ & = (\hat H_{el} |g\rangle) \otimes |n\rangle + |g\rangle \otimes (\hat H_{mot} |n\rangle)\\ & = (E_g |g\rangle) \otimes |n\rangle + |g\rangle \otimes (\hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) |n\rangle\\ & = (E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) |g,n\rangle \end{align} \tag{4.26} \]

Now using series expansion of the exponential, $e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar}$, we can write compactly:

\[ \ e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} \hat H_0 \Big)^k \tag{4.27} \]

So we may write:

\[ \begin{align} \ e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar} |g, n\rangle & = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} \hat H_0 \Big)^k |g, n\rangle \\ & = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} (E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) \Big)^k |g, n\rangle \\ & = e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t/\hbar} |g, n\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.28} \]

So the integral becomes:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt'= -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \Big]\\ \end{align} \tag{4.29} \]

The next step is to let $\hat a$ and $\hat a^\dagger$ operate on $|g, n\rangle$. The ladder operators only affect the motional states $|n\rangle$, follwowing equations 3.34 and 3.35. After some rearranging, we end up with:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \cos(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \sin(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n+1} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \sin(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle dt' \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.30} \]

If we now rewrite $\sin(\omega t')$ and $\cos(\omega t')$ in exponential form, that is:

\[ \begin{align} \ \sin(\omega t') = \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \\ \cos(\omega t') = \frac{1}{2} \Big(e^{i \omega t'} + e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.31} \]

We can then subsituting these forms into equation 4.29 to get:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2} \Big(e^{i \omega t'} + e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n+1} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle dt' \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.32} \]

Combining the exponential terms gives:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} + e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt'\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.33} \]

We now define, $\alpha \equiv \frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})$. This gives a somewhat, but not by much, cleaner expression:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} + e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt'\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.34} \]

Finally, we are ready to integrate these expressions. Upon evaluating each integral from $0$ to $t$ and doing a bit of algebra, the result is:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{i}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.35} \]

The total wavefunction is then:

\[ \begin{align} \ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i \alpha t} |g,n\rangle & -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \frac{i}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} -1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} +1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.36} \]

Let's pause and assess the result. The first term of the wavefunction represents the free evolution under the time-independent Hamiltonian which indicates phase rotations of the state. The second term corresponds to transitions in which the phonon number, $n$, is unchanged known as the carrier transition. The third and fourth terms correspond to transitions in which the phonon number is decreased or increased by one, respectively. As stated above, these are known as the red and blue sideband transitions. Shortly, we will understand why we call them transitions in the first place but the labels "red" and "blue" make sense because in terms of wavelength/frequency, red is lower energy, one less phonon, while blue is higher energy, one additional phonon.

Additionally, the messy exponentials in brackets will determine which processes become resonant based on the values of $(\alpha \pm \omega)$. We will see that when the laser frequency matches one of these energy differences, the corresponding amplitude dominates. For now, we ignore these terms just to get a sence of the state dynamics.

We are now ready to understand each of these terms through projection. In other words, if the we start off in the state $|\psi(0)\rangle = |g,n\rangle$, then what is the amplitude for the system to be found in the state $|e,n\rangle$ at time $t$? Here, $|e\rangle$ represents the electronic excited state. We will explore other possibilities of course but let's see what happens in this case. To project, we take the inner product:

\[ \ c_{e,n}(t) \equiv \langle e,n | \psi(t)\rangle \tag{4.37} \]

For the first term, we see that we will have:

\[ \ c_{e,n}(t) \propto \langle e,n | g,n\rangle \tag{4.38} \]

To compute this we use the fact that the inner product on a tensor product space is defined so that:

\[ \ \Big( \langle a| \otimes \langle b |\Big)\Big(|c\rangle \otimes |d\rangle\Big) = \langle a|b \rangle \langle c|d \rangle \tag{4.39} \]

In our case, we have:

\[ \begin{align} \ \langle e,n | g,n \rangle & = \Big( \langle e| \otimes \langle n |\Big)\Big(|g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle\Big) \\ & = \langle e|g \rangle \langle n|n \rangle = 0 \\ \end{align} \tag{4.40} \]

The free evolution cannot produce population in $|e,n\rangle$ due to the orthogonality of $|e\rangle$ and $|g\rangle$. Of course! Without interaction the system cannot change it's electronic state. If you proceed in a similar fashion you find that only the term second term, involving $|g,n\rangle$ survives! So that:

\[ \begin{align} \ \langle e,n | \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} |g,n\rangle & = \Big( \langle e| \otimes \langle n |\Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} \Big(|g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle\Big) \\ & = \Big( \langle e| \otimes \langle n |\Big) \Big(\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} |g\rangle \Big)|n\rangle \\ & = \langle e| \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0}|g\rangle \langle n|n \rangle \\ & = \langle e| \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0}|g\rangle \end{align} \tag{4.41} \]

Noting that the electric dipole operator only applies to the internal electronic states. It is recommended to show that the third and fourth terms are zero due to the orthogonality of $|n\rangle$ with $|n-1\rangle$ and $|n+1\rangle$, respectively.

So, we can write compactly:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle \propto \langle e|\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} | g \rangle \tag{4.42} \]

Or exlicitly:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \tag{4.43} \]

Now, the unperturbed Hamiltonian is still given by equation 4.24. Note that $\hat H_{el}|e\rangle = E_e|e\rangle$ so following the structure of equations 4.26:

\[ \ \hat H_0 |e,n\rangle = \Big(E_e + \hbar \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2})\Big)|e,n\rangle \tag{4.44} \]

As previously shown, this leads to:

\[ \ e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |e,n\rangle = e^{-i\Big(E_e + \hbar \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2})\Big)t/\hbar}|e,n\rangle \tag{4.45} \]

Which is the free evolution of the excited state. For convenience, we let $\beta = \frac{E_e}{\hbar} + \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2})$, which represents the natural frequency of the excited state. Recall that $\alpha = \frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2})$ represents the natural frequency of the ground state. From these relations we may write the frequency difference between the ground and excited state as:

\[ \begin{align} \ \beta - \alpha & = \frac{E_e}{\hbar} + \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2}) - \frac{E_g}{\hbar} - \omega_z(n + \frac{1}{2}) \\ & = \frac{E_e - E_g}{\hbar} \equiv \omega_0 \end{align} \tag{4.46} \]

where $\omega_0$ is the natural frequency difference of the transition from ground to excited state. We now rewrite $\alpha$ as:

\[ \ \alpha = \beta - \omega_0 \tag{4.47} \]

So, when we insert this back into equation 4.43 we get:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{1}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\beta - \omega_0 - \omega)t} - 1}{\beta - \omega_0 - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\beta -\omega_0 + \omega)t} - 1}{\beta - \omega_0 + \omega}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \tag{4.48} \]

Now we have the projection in terms of the frequency difference instead. After some factoring, our result becomes:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{e^{-i \beta t}}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{i(\omega_0 + \omega)t} - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta - \omega_0 - \omega} + \frac{e^{i(\omega_0 - \omega)t} - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta - \omega_0 + \omega}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \tag{4.49} \]

Let's focus on the what happens when $\omega = \omega_0$. We can see that:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle = \frac{e^{-i \beta t}}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{i 2 \omega_0 t} - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta - 2\omega_0} + \frac{1 - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \tag{4.50} \]

The first term, $\frac{e^{i 2 \omega_0 t} - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta - 2\omega_0}$, oscillates rapidly at optical frequencies and remains bounded in magnitude. It does not accumulate coherently. In first-order perturbation theory, such rapidly rotating terms are neglected.

To examine the time dependence of the second term, $\frac{1 - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta}$, we expand the exponential to first order in $t$:

\[ \ \frac{1 - e^{i \beta t}}{\beta} \approx \frac{1 - [1 + i \beta t]}{\beta} = -it \tag{4.51} \]

So, we get a linear build up which accumulates. Finally, for the carrier transition we have:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle \approx -\frac{it}{2 \hbar} \langle e|\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0}|g \rangle \tag{4.52} \]

At resonance, the oscillatory phase associated with the frequency difference vanishes, so this linear growth is not suppressed by destructive interference and can continue to accumulate. Neglecting the rapidly oscillating term near resonance is known as the rotating wave approximation (RWA). The rapidly oscillating term remains bounded and does not accumulate coherently. Neglecting such counter-rotating terms near resonance is known as the rotating wave approximation. This approximation is valid when the optical frequency is much larger than the coupling strength, which is the case for typical trapped-ion systems.

Now we can determine the amplitudes for the red and blue sideband transitions. Again, assuming we start off in the ground state, $|\psi(0)\rangle = |g,n\rangle$, we'd like to see what is the amplitude for the system to be in the $|e, n-1\rangle$ state or the $|e, n + 1\rangle$ state at some time $t$. That is:

\[ \begin{align} \ c_{e,n-1}(t) & = \langle e,n - 1 | \psi(t)\rangle \\ c_{e, n+1}(t) & = \langle e, n + 1 |\psi(t)\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.53} \]

Using the same orthogonality argument from earlier, the only terms which survive the respective projections are:

\[ \begin{align} \ c_{e,n-1}(t) & = -i \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \\ c_{e, n+1}(t) & = -i \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2 \hbar} \Big[\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}\Big] \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.54} \]

Following the same logic as above we can show that the free evolution phases of the excited states corresponding to each sideband are:

\[ \begin{align} \ \beta_{n-1} = \frac{E_e}{\hbar} + \omega_z(n - \frac{1}{2}) \\ \beta_{n+1} = \frac{E_e}{\hbar} + \omega_z(n + \frac{3}{2}) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.55} \]

Just as before, we are interested in the frequency difference. Rewriting $\alpha$ now has an additional term in each sideband phase:

\[ \begin{align} \ \alpha = \beta_{n-1} -\omega_0 + \omega_z \\ \alpha = \beta_{n+1} -\omega_0 -\omega_z \\ \end{align} \tag{4.56} \]

Examining the cases where $\omega = \omega_0 - \omega_z$ and $\omega = \omega_0 + \omega_z$ follows the same analysis as shown above for the carrier transition. Therefore, it follows that the resulting amplitudes for the red and blue sideband are:

\[ \begin{align} \ \langle e,n - 1 | \psi(t)\rangle &\approx \frac{-i t \eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2 \hbar} \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \\ \langle e, n + 1 |\psi(t)\rangle &\approx \frac{-i t \eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2 \hbar} \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.57} \]

So from the Schrodinger equation and a time-dependent interaction, we have shown, to first order, that their are 3 separate processes which can take place. A carrier transition responsible for promotion from ground to excited state with constant phonon number, a red sideband transition responsible for excitation from ground to excited states with a reduction in the phonon number and a blue sideband transition responsible for ground to excited state transitions involving an increase in phonon number. It is important to note that each amplitude clearly depends on the dipole moment operator and the electric field amplitude and polarization. We will not discuss much further about these but in real systems these quantities play an important role for the transition probability.

Two-Level Systems (Schrodinger Picture)

First-order perturbation theory reveals the resonance conditions and initial linear growth of the transition amplitude. However, perturbation theory breaks down once the excited-state population becomes appreciable. To understand coherent population transfer and pulse control, we must now solve the dynamics exactly at resonance.

At resonance and under the rotating wave approximation (RWA), only the resonant terms survive. For the carrier transition, we found that:

\[ \ \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle \approx -\frac{it}{2 \hbar} \langle e|\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0}|g \rangle \tag{4.58} \]

We define:

\[ \ \hbar \Omega \equiv \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} |g \rangle \tag{4.59} \]

Where $\Omega = \frac{1}{\hbar} \langle e |\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E_0} |g \rangle$ is known as the Rabi Frequency

So that:

\[ \ c_{e,n}(t) = -\frac{i \Omega t}{2} \tag{4.60} \]

The Schrodinger Equation tells us that:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle = |\psi(0)\rangle - \frac{i}{\hbar} \hat H_{eff} t |\psi(0)\rangle + \mathcal O (t^2) \tag{4.61} \]

So that when we project onto to $|e,n\rangle$:

\[ \ c_{e,n}(t) = \langle e,n|\psi(t)\rangle = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \langle e,n| \hat H_{eff}|g,n\rangle t \tag{4.62} \]

Of course, equation 4.60 and 4.62 are equivalent. Then:

\[ \ \langle e,n| \hat H_{eff}|g,n\rangle = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \tag{4.63} \]

We desire to write the effective Hamiltonian, $\hat H_{eff}$, in the simplest possible form which couples the ground state $|g,n\rangle$ to the excited state $|e,n\rangle$.

Verify that the effective Hamiltonian can be written as:

\[ \ \hat H_{eff} = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \Big(|e,n\rangle \langle g,n| + |g,n\rangle \langle e,n| \Big) \tag{4.64} \]

Since all other states are irrelevant for the carrier transition, the Hilbert space collapses to a two-dimensional subspace. We can express the wavefunction in this two dimensional subspace as a superposition:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle = c_{g,n}(t) |g,n\rangle + c_{e,n}(t)|e,n\rangle \tag{4.65} \]

Plugging this into the Schrodinger equation for our effective Hamiltonian we obtain two coupled differential equations:

\[ \begin{align} \ i \dot c_{g,n}(t) & = \frac{\Omega}{2} c_{e,n}(t) \\ i \dot c_{e,n}(t) & = \frac{\Omega}{2} c_{g,n}(t) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.66} \]

If we now differentiate again and plug in $\dot c_{g,n}(t)$ into the second expression, we get:

\[ \ \ddot c_{e,n}(t) = -\frac{\Omega^2}{4} c_{e,n}(t) \tag{4.67} \]

Which is simple harmonic oscillation with solutions of the form:

\[ \ c_{e,n}(t) = A \sin (\frac{\Omega t}{2}) + B \cos (\frac{\Omega t}{2}) \tag{4.68} \]

Using the intial conditions, starting in the ground state $c_{g,n}(0) = 1$ and $c_{e,n}(0) = 0$ we arrive at:

\[ \begin{align} \ c_{e,n}(t) = -i \sin(\frac{\Omega t}{2}) \\ c_{g,n}(t) = \cos(\frac{\Omega t}{2}) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.69} \]

Plugging these back into equation 4.65, the wavefunction is then:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle = \cos(\frac{\Omega t}{2}) |g,n\rangle - i \sin(\frac{\Omega t}{2}) |e,n\rangle \tag{4.70} \]

The excited state population probability is then the square modulus of the excited state amplitude:

\[ \ P_{e,n}(t) = |c_{e,n}(t)|^2 = \sin^2 (\frac{\Omega t}{2}) \tag{4.71} \]

Therefore, the population oscillates coherently between the ground state to the excited states at the Rabi frequency. (b) Consider the same system but now compute the excited state probability if the pulse duration is $t_{\pi/2} = \frac{\pi}{2 \Omega}$

In the last exercise, an equal superposition is created by using a $\pi/2$ pulse duration. This is how qubits are created and manipulated in trapped-ion systems.

Single Qubit Rotation Examples

If we analyze the effective Hamiltonian of the carrier transition from earlier:

\[ \ \hat H_{eff} = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \Big(|e,n\rangle \langle g,n| + |g,n\rangle \langle e,n| \Big) \tag{4.72} \]

We now assign the states: $|g,n\rangle \equiv |0\rangle$ and $|e,n\rangle \equiv |1\rangle$ so that:

\[ \ \hat H_{eff} = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \Big(|1\rangle \langle 0| + |0\rangle \langle 1| \Big) \tag{4.73} \]

Using the standard basis, we can express $|1\rangle \langle 0| + |0\rangle \langle 1|$ explicitly as: $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ \end{bmatrix}$

This is precisely the Pauli-$X$ matrix which we now denote as, $\sigma_x$. So we can write our effective Hamiltonian as:

\[ \ \hat H_{eff} = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \sigma_x \tag{4.74} \]

The time evolution operator is then:

\[ \ \hat U(t) = e^{-i \hat H_{eff} t/\hbar} = e^{-i \frac{\Omega t}{2} \sigma_x} \tag{4.75} \]

And this is a standard rotation operator commonly expressed as:

\[ \ \hat U(t) \Rightarrow R_x (\theta) = e^{-i \frac{\theta}{2} \sigma_x} \tag{4.76} \]

where $\theta = \Omega t$. So while the Hamiltonian generates continuous coherent rotations (Rabi oscillations), a pulse duration of $\pi$-pulses produces a produces a bit flip via state population change. The Hamiltonian acts like the Pauli-$X$ gates we saw in the Postulate 2 section.

In general, if the laser carries a controllable phase $\phi$, the effective Hamiltonian becomes:

\[ \ \hat H_{eff} = \frac{\hbar \Omega}{2} \Big(\cos \phi \sigma_x + \sin \phi \sigma_y \Big) \tag{4.77} \]

where $\sigma_y$ is the Pauli-$Y$ matrix. This generates rotations about an arbitrary axis in the equatorial plane of the Bloch sphere. Therefore, by controlling the pulse duration and phase of the laser, arbitrary single-qubit rotations can be implemented.

We have now identified the physical mechanism by which abstract single-qubit gates are realized in trapped-ion systems. Controlling the laser pulse duration determines the rotation because $t = \frac{\theta}{\Omega}$.

Sideband Cooling

In this section, we explain why the removal of a phonon during the red sideband transition is important for trapped-ion quantum computing. Before computing we must cool the ion because thermal motion reduces the fidelity of any computations. A standard cooling techniques known as Doppler Cooling cannot by itself reach the motional ground state because it is limited by the natural linewidth of the transition. Sideband cooling overcomes this limit by selectively removing individual phonons. Doppler Cooling and Sideband Cooling are used in conjunction to achieve ground state cooling. We require that $|n\rangle \rightarrow |0\rangle$. This is important for entangling gates, described later, as these gates require a well-defined motional state.

Recall that the effective Hamiltonian for the red sideband is:

\[ \ \hat H_{red} = \frac{\hbar \Omega \eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2} \Big(|e,n -1 \rangle \langle g,n| + |g,n\rangle \langle e, n - 1| \Big) \tag{4.78} \]

Using the probability of the excited state from exercise 4.2, a $\pi$-pulse performs $|g,n\rangle \rightarrow |e,n-1\rangle$. Thus, removing one phonon while exciting the atom. After excitation, it is possible for the atom to spontaneously decay such that $|e\rangle \rightarrow |g\rangle$. Two important factors to note are:

1. Spontaneous decay is fast compared to the motional dynamics.

2. In the Lamb-Dicke regime, the spatial extent of the ion’s motion is much smaller than the photon wavelength. As a result, recoil does not significantly change the phonon number.

This means we can achieve $|e,n -1 \rangle \rightarrow |g, n - 1 \rangle$ which is the cooling step.

Putting it all together, the net effect of a red sideband $\pi$-pulse is:

\[ \ |g,n\rangle \rightarrow |g, n - 1\rangle \tag{4.79} \]

By repeatedly applying red sideband excitation followed by spontaneous decay, phonons are removed one by one. Continuing this process eventually leads to $|g,0\rangle$. Of course, there is a limit imposed by the annihilation operator! Notice that the red sideband frequency is:

\[ \ \Omega_{red} = \Omega \eta_z \sqrt{n} \tag{4.80} \]

If $n = 0$, then $\Omega_{red} = 0$. The red sideband cannot drive $|g,0\rangle \rightarrow |e,-1\rangle$ because $\hat a|0\rangle = 0$. Therefore motional ground state is becomes a dark state of red sideband interaction, and cooling naturally stops. Remember, our major assumption is that spontaneous emission does not causes changes to $n$ because we are considering the Lamb-Dicke regime, $\eta_z \ll 1$. That is, if the ion’s motion is small compared to the photon wavelength, recoil is suppressed. Otherwise spontaneous emission would reheat the ion.

Preparing the ion in the $|g, 0\rangle$ state ensures that the shared motional mode begins in a well-defined quantum state, which is essential for high-fidelity entangling gates.

Jaynes-Cummings Model

The red and blue sideband Hamiltonians derived above describe a two-level system coupled to a quantized harmonic oscillator. This structure is not unique to trapped-ion physics; it is the canonical interaction studied in quantum optics and is known as the Jaynes–Cummings model. In fact, without assuming it a priori, we have derived this interaction directly from the laser–ion coupling in the Lamb–Dicke regime. We now recast the sideband Hamiltonian in its familiar Jaynes–Cummings form to connect our derivation with the standard framework used throughout modern quantum optics and quantum information theory.

Starting with the Hamiltonian of the red sideband, we now remove the fixed-$n$ assumption and restore the oscillator operators $\hat a$ and $\hat a^\dagger$. The tensor products in equation 4.78 can be factored as:

\[ \begin{align} \ |e, n - 1\rangle & = |e\rangle \otimes |n - 1\rangle \\ |g, n \rangle & = |g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.81} \]

So that:

\[ \begin{align} \ |e, n - 1\rangle \langle g, n| & = |e\rangle \langle g| \otimes |n - 1\rangle \langle n| \\ |g, n \rangle \langle e, n - 1| & = |g\rangle \langle e| \otimes |n\rangle \langle n - 1|\\ \end{align} \tag{4.82} \]

Which we will replace in equation 4.78. Now, recall that the annihilation operator acting on $|n\rangle$ is:

\[ \ \hat a | n \rangle = \sqrt{n} |n - 1\rangle \tag{4.83} \]

From this, we could write the operator as:

\[ \ \hat a = \sqrt{n} |n - 1\rangle \langle n | \tag{4.84} \]

You can check for yourself that this works by applying it directly to $|n\rangle$. Now, equation 4.78 reads:

\[ \ \hat H_{red} = \frac{\hbar \Omega \eta_z }{2} \Big(|e\rangle \langle g| \otimes \underbrace{\sqrt{n} |n - 1\rangle \langle n|}_{\hat a} + |g\rangle \langle e| \otimes \underbrace{\sqrt{n} |n\rangle \langle n - 1|}_{\hat a^\dagger} \Big) \tag{4.85} \]

Where we have used the Hermitian conjugate of $\hat a$ for the second term. Similar to above, we define $|g\rangle \equiv |0\rangle$ and $|e\rangle \equiv |1\rangle$ so that:

\[ \ \hat H_{red} = \frac{\hbar \Omega \eta_z }{2} \Big( \hat a |1\rangle \langle 0| + \hat a^\dagger |0\rangle \langle 1| \Big) \tag{4.86} \]

In standard quantum optics, we define $\sigma_+ \equiv |1\rangle \langle 0|$ and $\sigma_- \equiv |0\rangle \langle 1|$ so we write:

\[ \ \hat H_{red} = \hbar g \Big( \hat a \sigma_+ + \hat a^\dagger \sigma_- \Big) \tag{4.87} \]

where $g = \frac{\Omega \eta_z}{2}$. In this next excercise you will show that the blue sideband Hamiltonian can be written in a similar way.

Show that the blue sideband Hamiltonian can be written as: $\hat H_{blue} = \hbar g \Big( \hat a^\dagger \sigma_+ + \hat a \sigma_- \Big) $

The red and blue sideband Hamiltonians therefore take the form of the Jaynes–Cummings and anti–Jaynes–Cummings models, respectively. This reveals that trapped-ion systems implement the same fundamental light–matter interaction studied throughout quantum optics: a two-level system coherently exchanging excitations with a quantized harmonic oscillator. By tuning the laser frequency, we selectively activate either excitation exchange (red sideband) or joint excitation creation (blue sideband), while the carrier interaction remains a pure two-level rotation.
