Laser-Ion Interaction: Coupling Internal States to Motion

The Lamb-Dicke Parameter

Consider the general form of the propagating electric field of a laser:

\[ \ \mathbf{E} (\mathbf{r},t) \propto \cos (\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} - \omega t) \tag{4.1} \]

where $\mathbf{k}$ is the wave vector, $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector, and $\omega$ is the laser frequency. We start by considering axial coupling only which means that $\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{r} = kz$. And since we know that in quantum mechanics the position is an operator, $z \rightarrow \hat Z$, then:

\[ \ \mathbf{E} (\hat Z,t) \propto \cos (k_z \hat Z - \omega t) \tag{4.2} \]

Now, for a charge distribution in an external electric field, the interaction energy is given from classical electromagnetism:

\[ \ U = - \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{E} \tag{4.3} \]

where $\mathbf{p}$ is the electric dipole moment. Since the electron cloud surrounding the atom moves in response to the external electric field we express the dipole moment as $\mathbf{p} = -e \mathbf{r}_e$ where $e$ is the charge and $\mathbf{r}_e$ is the electrons position relative to the nucleus. In quantum mechanics, the dipole moment becomes an operator acting on the internal electronic states, while the electric field must be evaluated at the ion’s position operator. The resulting time-dependent interaction Hamiltonian therefore couples the ion’s internal states to its quantized motional states.

\[ \ \hat H_{int} (t) = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(k_z \hat Z - \omega t) \tag{4.4} \]

where $\mathbf{E}_0$ is the electric field amplitude. Now recall the expression for the position operator $\hat Z$ from the last section.

\[ \ \hat Z = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger \big) \tag{4.5} \]

So that:

\[ \ \hat H_{int} (t) = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos \big (k_z \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger \big ) - \omega t \big ) \tag{4.6} \]

We now define the Lamb-Dicke parameter as:

\[ \ \eta_z \equiv k_z \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \tag{4.7} \]

In what's known as the Lamb-Dicke regime the ion's spatial extent is much smaller than the laser wavelength $\lambda$ and since $k_z \sim \frac{1}{\lambda}$, it follows that in the Lamb-Dicke regime:

\[ \ \eta_z \ll 1 \tag{4.8} \]

Under this approximation, we may expand the cosine term of the interaction Hamiltonian as:

\[ \ \cos \big( \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) - \omega t \big) \approx \cos(\omega t) - \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t) + \mathcal O (\eta_z^2) \tag{4.9} \]

where $\mathcal O(\eta_z^2)$ represents higher order terms in the expansion. The zeroth-order term, $\cos(\omega t)$, corresponds to laser-driven internal transitions that do not affect the ion’s motional state. Note that motion is invisible to the laser, the ion behaves point-like, and the motional quantum number $n$ does not change. This is known as the carrier transition. The first-order term, $ \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t)$ accounts for the finite spatial extent of the ion’s motion and allows the laser to exchange energy with the motional degree of freedom. We already know that $\hat a^\dagger$ adds one phonon and $\hat a$ removes one phonon. This terms also informs us that the internal and motional energies are coupled. Additionally, the creation and annihilation of phonons correspond to what are known as blue and red sidebands, respectively. We will explore these in detail next. Lastly, the higher-order terms, $\mathcal O (\eta_z^2)$ correspond to multi-phonon processes and are suppressed by higher powers of $\eta_z$ in the Lamb–Dicke regime.

In this next section, we will go into a detailed derviation of the carrier transition and the red/blue sideband transitions to fully understand their origin (which technically is just the interaction Hamiltonian). It is highly encouraged to the reader to slow down and perform all necessary steps to reach the final result. We begin by considering the Schrodinger Equation of Postulate 2:

\[ \ i \hbar \frac{d}{dt} | \psi(t) \rangle = \hat H (t) |\psi (t) \rangle \tag{4.10} \]

We now split the Hamiltonian into a time-independent part, $\hat H_0$, and a time-dependent part, $\hat H_{int}$, so that:

\[ \ \hat H(t) = \hat H_0 + \hat H_{int} (t) \tag{4.11} \]

By plugging this into equation 4.10:

\[ \begin{align} \ i \hbar \frac{d}{dt}| \psi(t) \rangle & = (\hat H_0 + \hat H_{int} (t)) |\psi(t)\rangle \\ & = \hat H_0 |\psi(t)\rangle + \hat H_{int}(t) |\psi(t)\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.12} \]

Integrating both sides from $0$ to $t$ gives:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.13} \]

Focusing on just the first integral, equations 1.60 and 1.62 allows us to write the solution as:

\[ \ |\psi(t')\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle + \mathcal O (\hat H_{int}) \tag{4.14} \]

Therefore,

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle dt' \tag{4.15} \]

From the Schrodinger equation, $\hat H_0 e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/ \hbar} = i \hbar \frac{d}{dt'} |\psi(t')\rangle$, so:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t i \hbar \frac{d}{dt'} |\psi(t')\rangle |\psi(0)\rangle dt' \tag{4.16} \]

Which upon integrating gives:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_0 |psi(t')\rangle dt' = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{4.17} \]

So, from equation 4.13, we have:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - |\psi(0)\rangle - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.18} \]

Leading to:

\[ \ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i \hat H_0 t/\hbar} |\psi(0)\rangle - \frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \tag{4.18} \]

Now we can tackle the second integral which is much more formidable.

The interaction Hamiltonian under our approximation in equation 4.9 to first order becomes:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_{int} (t) & = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 [\cos(\omega t) - \eta_z (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) \sin(\omega t)] \\ & = - \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t) + \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t) + \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.19} \]

We now have 3 separate integrals to evaluate:

\[ \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')|\psi(t')\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')|\psi(t')\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.20} \]

Now since $|\psi(t') = e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle$, the integral is written:

\[ \ = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|\psi(0)\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.21} \]

We now take the intial state $|\psi(0)\rangle$ to be composite of electronic ground and motional states, $|g\rangle$ and $|n\rangle$, so that $|\psi(0)\rangle = |g,n\rangle$ where $|g,n \rangle$ is the tensor product from Posutlate 4: $|g,n\rangle = |g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle$. Therefore,

\[ \ = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle dt' \Big] \tag{4.22} \]

It is now important to recognize the action of $e^{-i \hat H_0 t'/\hbar}|g,n\rangle$ because it appears in each term. We first write the unperturbed Hamiltonian, $\hat H_0$ as the sum of an electronic Hamiltonian, $\hat H_{el}$, and a motional Hamiltonian, $\hat H_{mot}$, so that:

\[ \ \hat H_0 = \hat H_{el} + \hat H_{mot} \tag{4.23} \]

We already know that:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_{el} |g\rangle & = E_g |g\rangle \\ \hat H_{mot} |n\rangle & = \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})|n\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.24} \]

So, explicitly,

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H_0 |g,n\rangle & = (\hat H_{el} + \hat H_{mot})(|g\rangle \otimes |n\rangle)\\ & = (\hat H_{el} |g\rangle) \otimes |n\rangle + |g\rangle \otimes (\hat H_{mot} |n\rangle)\\ & = (E_g |g\rangle) \otimes |n\rangle + |g\rangle \otimes (\hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) |n\rangle\\ & = (E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) |g,n\rangle \end{align} \tag{4.25} \]

Now using series expansion of the exponential, $e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar}$, we can write compactly:

\[ \ e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} \hat H_0 \Big)^k \tag{4.26} \]

So we may write:

\[ \begin{align} \ e^{-i \hat H_0 t / \hbar} |g, n\rangle & = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} \hat H_0 \Big)^k |g, n\rangle \\ & = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \Big(-\frac{it}{\hbar} (E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2})) \Big)^k |g, n\rangle \\ & = e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t/\hbar} |g, n\rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{4.27} \]

So the integral becomes:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt'= -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \cos(\omega t')e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z \hat a \sin(\omega t')e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \int_0^t \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 \eta_z\hat a^\dagger \sin(\omega t') e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} |g, n\rangle dt' \Big]\\ \end{align} \tag{4.28} \]

The next step is to let $\hat a$ and $\hat a^\dagger$ operate on $|g, n\rangle$. The ladder operators only affect the motional states $|n\rangle$, follwowing equations 3.34 and 3.35. After some rearranging, we end up with:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \cos(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \sin(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n+1} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \sin(\omega t') \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle dt' \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.29} \]

If we now rewrite $\sin(\omega t')$ and $\cos(\omega t')$ in exponential form, that is:

\[ \begin{align} \ \sin(\omega t') = \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{-i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \\ \cos(\omega t') = \frac{1}{2} \Big(e^{-i \omega t'} + e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \\ \end{align} \tag{4.30} \]

We can then subsituting these forms into equation 4.29 to get:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2} \Big(e^{-i \omega t'} + e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{-i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle dt' \\ & + \eta_z \sqrt{n+1} \int_0^t e^{-i(E_g + \hbar \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}))t'/\hbar} \frac{1}{2i} \Big(e^{-i \omega t'} - e^{-i \omega t'} \Big) \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle dt' \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.31} \]

Combining the exponential terms gives:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} + e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}) + \omega)t'}] dt'\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.32} \]

We now define, $alpha \equiv \frac{E_g}{\hbar} + \omega_z (n + \frac{1}{2}). This gives a somewhat, but not by much, cleaner expression:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{1}{2} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} + e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt' \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} \int_0^t [e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'}] dt'\mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.33} \]

Finally, we are ready to integrate these expressions. Upon evaluating each integral from $0$ to $t$ and doing a bit of algebra, the result is:

\[ \begin{align} \ -\frac{i}{\hbar} \int_0^t \hat H_{int}(t') |\psi(t')\rangle dt' = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{i}{2} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} +1}{\alpha + \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} -1}{\alpha - \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} -1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} +1}{\alpha + \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.34} \]

The total wavefunction is then:

\[ \begin{align} \ \psi(t)\rangle = e^{-i \alpha t} |psi(0)\rangle \\ & = -\frac{i}{\hbar} \Big[ & - \frac{i}{2} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} +1}{\alpha + \omega} + \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} -1}{\alpha - \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n}}{2} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} - 1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} + 1}{\alpha + \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n-1\rangle \\ & + \frac{\eta_z \sqrt{n+1}}{2i} [\frac{e^{-i(\alpha - \omega)t'} -1}{\alpha - \omega} - \frac{e^{-i(\alpha + \omega)t'} +1}{\alpha + \omega}] \mathbf{\hat p} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 |g, n+1\rangle \Big] \\ \end{align} \tag{4.3} \]
